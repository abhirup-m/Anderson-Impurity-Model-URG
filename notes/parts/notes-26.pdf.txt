2.3.3

Some conclusions and observations

â€¢ The mean field solution predicts that local moments are sustained in the limit of
large ğ‘ˆ and small |ğœ–ğ‘‘ |. However, this treatment becomes faulty at low temperatures.
â€¢ At low temperatures, the resistivity is found to reach a minimum and then vary
as ln ğ‘‡ . This behavior stops at some very low temperature ğ‘‡ğ¾ . The temperature ğ‘‡ğ¾
is also that at which the magnetization vanishes, and the susceptibility becomes
constant, suggesting that the impurity spin has condensed into a singlet.

â€¢ Since the disappearance of the ln ğ‘‡ behavior is coincident with the condensation of
the spin degree of freedom, it is natural to hope that the resistivity minimum is a
result of the interaction between the impurity and the conduction spins.
â€¢ To describe such an interaction, the way to proceed is to strip the model of the
charge excitations (via a Schrieffer-Wolff transformation). The resultant Hamiltonian consists of an anti-ferromagnetic interaction between the itinerant spins and
the impurity spin, and is called the Kondo model.
23

2.4. THE KONDO MODEL
â€¢ Calculating the scattering rate up to second order using the Kondo model produces
a logarithmic term, which explains the log-dependence. Since this perturbative
treatment will fail at small temperatures (where the log term diverges), we need
some other technique to find out the fate of the model at low temperatures.
â€¢ Andersonâ€™s poor manâ€™s scaling wraps the effects of high energy scatterings into the
low energy model, showing that the anti-ferromagnetic coupling diverges at low
temperatures, producing a singlet.
â€¢ There are two routes that one can follow to note the changes in the system; one
is by reducing the temperature which is equivalent to folding in the high energy
fluctuations, aka scaling. The other is to reduce the onsite interaction ğ‘ˆ and note
the changes in state.
â€¢ Reducing the temperature or performing the RG takes the model from the Anderson model (ğ‘‡ > 0) to the Fermi liquid state (ğ‘‡ âˆ¼ ğ‘‡ğ¾ ). This Fermi liquid may have
interactions, depending on the value of ğ‘ˆ we are working in.
â€¢ Coming down to ğ‘‡ < ğ‘‡ğ¾ , we can now modify the ğ‘ˆ from âˆ to 0. Large ğ‘ˆ means the
Fermi liquid has large interactions. Reducing ğ‘ˆ means coming down to a Fermi
gas. For ğ‘‡ â‰  0, reducing ğ‘ˆ means going from local moment regime to non-magnetic
regime. For ğ‘‡ = 0, local moments persist for all ğ‘ˆ > 0.
â€¢ It will be seen that in the large ğ‘ˆ regime, the singlet channel scattering phase shift
